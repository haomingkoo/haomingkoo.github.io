What is Reinforcement Learning?

Before we can understand how AI models are aligned, we need to understand the learning framework behind it all: reinforcement learning.

Think about training a dog. You give a command, the dog does something, and you respond with a treat or a correction. Over time, the dog learns which actions earn rewards. This is reinforcement learning in a nutshell.

Section 2

How Does RL Apply to Language Models?

The jump from slot machines to ChatGPT is shorter than you think. Let's map the RL concepts onto language models.

A language model generates text one token at a time. When we want it to produce helpful, honest, and harmless responses, we need a way to tell it "this response was good" or "that response was bad." That is exactly what RL does.

The key difference: In traditional RL, rewards come from the environment (score in a game, distance traveled). For language models, there is no natural "score" - a response is not obviously +1 or -1. So the reward has to come from human feedback. That is what RLHF stands for - Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback.

The breakthrough: DPO shows that you can rearrange the math of RLHF to get a simple loss function that operates directly on preference pairs. No reward model. No RL. No instability. Just compare the model's log-probabilities on chosen vs. rejected responses, and backpropagate.
Section 5

The DPO Loss Function

One equation replaces the entire RLHF pipeline. Here is what it means and how it works.

Step-by-Step Derivation
Step 1: The RLHF Objective

Maximize reward while staying close to the reference policy:

$$\max_{\pi_\theta} \;\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D},\, y \sim \pi_\theta}\!\left[r(x, y)\right] - \beta \,\text{KL}\!\left[\pi_\theta(y|x) \,\|\, \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)\right]$$
This is the standard RLHF objective. The KL term prevents the model from drifting too far from the SFT model - without it, the model could "hack" the reward by producing degenerate text.
Step 2: Closed-Form Optimal Policy

This KL-constrained problem has a known closed-form solution:

$$\pi^*(y \mid x) = \frac{1}{Z(x)} \pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x) \exp\!\left(\frac{1}{\beta} r(x, y)\right)$$
The optimal policy is the reference policy scaled by exponentiated reward. $Z(x)$ is the partition function (a normalization constant that is intractable to compute).
Step 3: Rearrange for Reward

Solve the previous equation for $r(x, y)$:

$$r(x, y) = \beta \log \frac{\pi^*(y \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x)} + \beta \log Z(x)$$
Key insight: The reward is fully determined by the log-ratio of optimal policy to reference policy, plus a prompt-dependent constant $Z(x)$. The reward is hiding inside the policy all along.
Step 4: Substitute into Bradley-Terry - Z(x) Cancels!

The Bradley-Terry model says $P(y_w \succ y_l) = \sigma(r(x, y_w) - r(x, y_l))$. Substituting:

$$P(y_w \succ y_l) = \sigma\!\left(\beta \log \frac{\pi^*(y_w|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w|x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi^*(y_l|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l|x)}\right)$$

The $\beta \log Z(x)$ terms cancel because both responses share the same prompt!

The DPO loss follows directly: maximize the log-likelihood of human preferences under this model. No reward model. No RL. No partition function. Just classification.
$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}}(\pi_\theta; \pi_{\text{ref}}) = -\mathbb{E}\left[\log \sigma\!\left(\beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_w \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w \mid x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_l \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l \mid x)}\right)\right]$$

In plain language: make the model more likely to produce preferred responses and less likely to produce rejected ones, relative to how a frozen reference model would behave.

Implementation

DPO in PyTorch

The entire DPO loss fits in under 10 lines of code. Compare this to a full PPO implementation that typically runs hundreds of lines.

Python dpo_loss.py 
import torch.nn.functional as F

def dpo_loss(pi_logps_chosen, pi_logps_rejected,
             ref_logps_chosen, ref_logps_rejected, beta=0.1):
    """Direct Preference Optimization loss."""
    # Implicit rewards from log-ratios
    chosen_rewards = beta * (pi_logps_chosen - ref_logps_chosen)
    rejected_rewards = beta * (pi_logps_rejected - ref_logps_rejected)

    # DPO loss = -log sigmoid(reward_chosen - reward_rejected)
    loss = -F.logsigmoid(chosen_rewards - rejected_rewards).mean()
    return loss
That's it. No value function, no advantage estimation, no clipping, no reward model inference. The entire alignment signal comes from comparing log-probabilities of chosen vs rejected responses.

Section 7

Pros and Cons

DPO simplifies alignment but comes with its own trade-offs.

Advantages

  • No RL needed: pure supervised learning with a classification loss
  • Same theoretical objective: provably equivalent to RLHF under Bradley-Terry
  • Computationally lightweight: no reward model, no online sampling, ~1/3 compute
  • Stable training: no PPO clipping heuristics, no reward hacking
  • Simple to implement: fits in under 10 lines of PyTorch

Limitations

  • Offline only: uses fixed preference dataset, cannot explore new responses during training
  • Distribution shift: preference data may not cover the model's actual output distribution
  • Likelihood displacement: may increase probability of unseen responses as a side effect
  • Implicit reward generalization: unclear if the hidden reward transfers to out-of-distribution prompts
When to Use What
Choose DPO when...
  • You have a good preference dataset
  • Compute budget is limited
  • You want stable, reproducible training
  • Simple implementation is a priority
Consider RLHF when...
  • You need online exploration
  • Preference data is limited but you can generate more
  • You need the reward model for other purposes
  • Distribution shift is a concern
Discussion

The Big Question

"If RLHF and DPO both optimize for human preferences, are we baking human biases into AI?"

DPO makes alignment cheaper and more accessible. But the preference data reflects the values, blind spots, and biases of the annotators who created it.

When alignment becomes a simple classification loss, the barrier to deploying "aligned" models drops. Is that purely good?

The technical question is solved. The societal question is just beginning.

Summary

Where It Fits, What It Proves, What Comes Next

The Problem

RLHF is unstable, expensive, and complex. It requires training a separate reward model, running PPO, and keeping multiple models in memory.

Most teams lack the resources to do it properly.

The Contribution

  • DPO reformulates alignment as classification
  • One loss function, one hyperparameter ($\beta$)
  • Same theoretical objective as RLHF
  • Matches or exceeds PPO on all benchmarks tested
  • A fraction of the compute cost

Open Questions

  • Offline only - no exploration
  • Untested at largest scales (at time of publication)
  • Distribution shift with stale preference data
  • Bias amplification concerns
  • Likelihood displacement side effects
