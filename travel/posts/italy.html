13 Days Across Italy — A Winter Family Trip

This was a family trip — four of us, 13 days, five cities. We wanted to do Italy properly: comfortable hotels in good locations, sit-down meals, and enough time in each city to actually enjoy it instead of rushing through a checklist.

Our trip coincided with the start of the Catholic Jubilee Year 2025 — a once-every-25-years event where the Vatican opens the Holy Door (Porta Santa) at St. Peter's Basilica. Pope Francis officially opened it on Christmas Eve 2024, right when we were in Italy. We actually got to walk through the Holy Door ourselves — it's a simple doorway, but knowing it only opens once every 25 years made it feel really special. The Jubilee has since ended (January 2026), but I wanted to share what the experience was like. Rome had a different energy during this period — pilgrims from around the world, extra celebrations, and the city felt alive in a way that's hard to put into words. The next Jubilee won't be until 2050, but hopefully it'll be just as incredible.

I'm sharing this itinerary hoping it helps someone plan their own trip. We learned a lot along the way — especially about booking tickets (more on that later) — and if I can save you a few headaches, this post has done its job.

Winter Italy is a different experience from the summer crowds. The streets are quieter, the Christmas markets are magical, and you can actually stand in front of a painting at the Uffizi without someone's selfie stick in your peripheral vision. The weather is cool (5–12°C) but honestly perfect for walking — you don't sweat through 20,000 steps a day like you would in July.

Pro tip: Book trains on Trenitalia or Italo early. We scored Milan → Florence for about EUR 20 per person. That same route can cost EUR 60+ if you book last minute.

Milan

Milan 2 nights · Hilton Milan

We landed on Christmas Eve. Milan in December is cold, sharp, and impossibly stylish — everyone looks like they just walked out of a fashion editorial. The city was draped in Christmas lights and the Duomo looked like it was designed specifically to be a postcard. Fun fact: the Duomo took nearly 600 years to complete (1386–1965). They started building it before Columbus sailed to America and finished it after humans landed on the moon.

Day 1 — Dec 24
Arrival + Christmas Eve
Landed at Malpensa, checked into the Hilton, and walked to the Duomo for the Christmas Eve atmosphere. The piazza was packed with families, street performers, and the smell of roasted chestnuts. We grabbed panettone from a bakery — because when in Italy, you eat panettone, not log cake.
Arrival Christmas
Day 2 — Dec 25
Duomo, Sforza Castle, and The Last Supper
Christmas Day — most shops were closed, but the Duomo area still had crowds, buskers, and a festive atmosphere. We went up to the rooftop terraces — the view of the city with the Alps in the distance is something else. Then walked to Sforza Castle, which houses several museums (including unfinished Michelangelo works). The highlight: Cenacolo Vinciano — Leonardo da Vinci's The Last Supper. You get exactly 15 minutes in the room. Book months in advance or don't bother. We managed to grab a bite at one of the few open trattorias nearby — Christmas in Italy is more about family than commerce, so plan your meals ahead.
Duomo Da Vinci Culture
Day 3 — Dec 26
QC Termemilano + Train to Florence
Started the morning at QC Termemilano — a thermal spa built inside old Milan tram tunnels. Hot pools, steam rooms, and a rooftop jacuzzi overlooking the city. This was honestly one of the highlights of the entire trip. After two days of walking in the cold, sinking into a hot pool on a crisp December morning felt incredible. Highly recommend doing this on a day when most shops are still closed from Christmas — it's the perfect use of a quiet morning. Then we hopped on the Trenitalia to Florence. The train ride is about 1h 45m and the Tuscan countryside views are free.
Spa Train

Florence

Florence 3 nights · 25hours Hotel Florence

Florence is what happens when an entire city decides to be a museum. Every street corner has a building that's older than Singapore by about 500 years. The food is incredible — Florentine steak (bistecca alla fiorentina) is a thick-cut T-bone, barely seasoned, cooked rare over a wood fire. It's one of those dishes that makes you rethink everything you thought you knew about steak.

The leather shops are everywhere (Florence has been a leather-working city since the 1200s), and you will eat your body weight in gelato whether you planned to or not. Our hotel, the 25hours, had a great rooftop bar with views of the Duomo — perfect for winding down after a full day of walking.

Day 4 — Dec 27
Galleria dell'Accademia + Duomo di Firenze
Michelangelo's David is one of those things that looks impressive in photos but absolutely floors you in person. The statue is 5.17m tall and the level of detail is absurd — you can see the veins in his hands. After that, we climbed 463 steps to the top of the Duomo di Firenze. The Brunelleschi dome is an engineering marvel from the 1400s that still makes modern architects jealous.
Michelangelo Architecture
Day 5 — Dec 28
Day Trip to Pisa
Took the regional train to Pisa (about 1h each way, EUR 10 return). Yes, we did the obligatory "holding up the tower" photo. Yes, it was worth it. The Leaning Tower actually leans at 3.97 degrees — they've spent centuries trying to stabilise it. The entire Piazza dei Miracoli (Field of Miracles) is gorgeous. Half-day trip, back in Florence for a Florentine steak dinner.
Day Trip Pisa Food
Day 6 — Dec 29
Uffizi Gallery + Train to Rome
The Galleria degli Uffizi has Botticelli's Birth of Venus, Caravaggio, Raphael, and more Renaissance art than your brain can process in one visit. We spent 3 hours and barely scratched the surface. After lunch, boarded the train to Rome. Next stop: the Eternal City.
Art Uffizi Train

Rome

Rome 3 nights · Mercure Rome Colosseum Centre

Rome is chaos, beauty, and carbs all mixed together. The hotel was right next to the Colosseum — you could literally see it from the window, which never stopped being surreal. Every direction you walk leads to something ancient, crumbling, and absolutely magnificent. You'll accidentally stumble into a 2,000-year-old temple on the way to get coffee. That's just Rome being Rome.

Quick note on Roman pizza vs Neapolitan pizza: Roman is thin and crispy, Neapolitan is thick and chewy. Both are correct life choices. We had both. Multiple times.

Day 7 — Dec 30
Vatican Museum + Sistine Chapel
We did a guided tour of the Vatican Museum and Sistine Chapel. The museum has dozens of galleries and roughly 70,000 works of art — so a guide is essential unless you want to wander aimlessly for 6 hours (which is also valid, honestly). The Sistine Chapel ceiling is... there are no words. Michelangelo painted it over 4 years, working on scaffolding so high up that paint constantly dripped into his eyes. He reportedly wrote a poem about how miserable the experience was. But the result is one of the greatest achievements in human art. Go figure.
Vatican Michelangelo
Day 8 — Dec 31
Colosseum + New Year's Eve
Morning at the Colosseum with Attico (top level) access. Standing where gladiators fought 2,000 years ago is genuinely humbling. The engineering behind the arena — retractable awnings, underground tunnels for animals and fighters, trapdoors in the arena floor — was centuries ahead of its time. Fun fact: the Colosseum could seat 50,000 spectators and had 80 entrances. They could fill and empty the entire stadium in 15 minutes. Modern stadiums still struggle with that.
The ticket saga: Colosseum tickets with Attico access are notoriously hard to get. I was checking the official site every single day for weeks before the trip, refreshing at different times. They release tickets in batches and they sell out within minutes. I managed to snag tickets on my first day of trying — pure luck. More on this in the ticket guide below.
NYE was magical. We had dinner at a local trattoria, then did the countdown from the rooftop of our hotel. The fireworks were 360 degrees — literally every direction you looked, the sky was lit up. The view from our hotel room of the Colosseum with fireworks behind it is something I'll never forget. Rome on New Year's Eve is an experience I'd recommend to anyone.
Colosseum NYE
Day 9 — Jan 1
New Year's Day + Train to Venice
Slow morning. Walked around Trastevere and the Roman Forum. Most places were closed for New Year's, which meant the streets were empty and peaceful — a rare sight in Rome. Afternoon train to Venice, our final stop.
Exploring Train

Venice

Venice 2 nights · Hotel Saturnia & International

Venice doesn't feel real. It's a floating city where streets are water and taxis are boats. There are no cars — not a single one on the entire island. You will get lost. That's the point. Every wrong turn leads to a hidden piazza, a canal reflection, or a tiny bakery you'd never have found otherwise. The city is built on 118 small islands connected by over 400 bridges. We crossed a lot of them.

Day 10 — Jan 2
Gondola, Doge's Palace, St. Mark's
Started with a gondola ride through the narrow canals. There's something about gliding through Venice in silence (well, except for the gondolier's commentary) that makes you feel like you've stepped back in time. The gondolas have been around since the 11th century — and by Venetian law, they must all be painted black. Then the Doge's Palace — the Secret Itineraries Tour takes you behind the walls into hidden chambers, through the prison cells, and across the Bridge of Sighs. St. Mark's Basilica is gold everywhere. The Pala d'Oro altarpiece alone has over 1,900 gemstones. It's genuinely hard to process how much detail is packed into one building.
Gondola History
Day 11 — Jan 3
Getting Lost (On Purpose)
No plan. Just walked. Found a tiny cicchetti bar where the owner insisted we try everything. Crossed the Rialto Bridge at sunset. Watched the boats go by with a spritz in hand. Sometimes the best travel experiences are the ones you don't plan.
Exploring Food
Day 12–13 — Jan 4–5
Departure
Water taxi to Marco Polo Airport. Flight back to Singapore via transit. Arrived home with sore feet, a full camera roll, and already planning the next trip.
Departure

What I'd Do Differently

No trip is perfect, and looking back, there are a few things I'd change. Sharing these so you can learn from my mistakes.

Split the Vatican and Colosseum into separate days

We did the Vatican Museum + Sistine Chapel on one day and the Colosseum the next, which worked out fine. But honestly, if I had a fourth night in Rome, I'd give each a full relaxed day. The Vatican alone can take 4–5 hours if you actually want to absorb what you're seeing (it has 54 galleries!). And the Colosseum + Roman Forum + Palatine Hill is easily another full day. We combined the Colosseum with NYE festivities, which meant we rushed the Forum a bit.

The reason we crammed it was tickets. Colosseum Attico tickets are released in batches and sell out in minutes. I was checking the website every day, trying different dates, hoping something would open up. When I finally got tickets for Dec 31, I grabbed them immediately — even though it meant doubling up with NYE plans. If you have flexibility on dates, you'll have a much easier time spacing things out.

Book The Last Supper earlier

I almost missed this one. Tickets for Cenacolo Vinciano release about 2–3 months in advance and sell out within days. Only 30 people are allowed in the room at a time, for exactly 15 minutes. I got lucky with a last-minute cancellation drop — but I wouldn't count on that. Set a calendar reminder for the day tickets open.

More time in Florence (and Venice)

Florence could easily take 4–5 nights if you want to do it justice. Three nights meant we had to squeeze in the Accademia, Duomo climb, Uffizi, and the Pisa day trip. It was doable but we were moving fast. If you have the time, an extra night in Florence lets you explore the Oltrarno neighbourhood, visit the Boboli Gardens, or just slow down and enjoy a long lunch by the Arno River without watching the clock.

Same for Venice — two nights felt short. Venice rewards slow exploration: getting lost in the back alleys, sitting by a canal with a coffee, taking the vaporetto to Burano (the colourful island). If I did it again, I'd add a third night for both cities.

Ticket Booking Guide

This was the most stressful part of planning. Italian attractions have wildly different booking windows, and some sell out months in advance. Here's what I learned the hard way — hopefully this saves you some anxiety.

Attraction How Early to Book
The Last Supper (Milan) 2–3 months. Sells out in days. Only 30 people per 15-min slot. Check for last-minute cancellation drops — they do happen, usually 1–2 days before.
Duomo di Milano (rooftop) 1–2 weeks. Easier to get, but rooftop terrace tickets go faster than the cathedral-only ones.
QC Termemilano 1 week. Walk-ins possible on weekdays. Book ahead for weekends and holidays.
Galleria dell'Accademia (Florence) 2–4 weeks. Morning slots go first. Michelangelo's David is the main draw and the museum isn't huge, so 1.5–2 hours is enough.
Duomo di Firenze (dome climb) 2–4 weeks. The dome climb has timed entry. Book the earliest slot if you want fewer crowds on the 463-step climb.
Galleria degli Uffizi 2–4 weeks. Manageable in winter, but summer is another story. Book a specific time slot to skip the line.
Vatican Museum + Sistine Chapel 2–4 weeks. Book the first morning slot (7:30am if available). By 10am the queues are absurd. A guided tour is worth it — the museum is overwhelming otherwise.
Colosseum + Attico (Rome) Check daily, 1–2 months out. Attico (top level) tickets are released in small batches and sell out in minutes. Standard tickets are easier — 1–2 weeks is fine. Keep refreshing if you want Attico.
Doge's Palace (Venice) 1–2 weeks. The Secret Itineraries Tour has limited spots but isn't as competitive as Rome/Florence attractions.
Gondola Ride (Venice) No booking needed. Walk to any gondola station and negotiate. Standard rate is EUR 80 per gondola (seats up to 6), so splitting with family/friends makes it very reasonable.
General tip: Always buy from the official website of each attraction. Third-party resellers charge 2–3x the price for the same ticket. Set calendar reminders for when tickets open. And check for last-minute drops — people cancel, especially 1–2 days before. I scored The Last Supper tickets this way.

Practical Tips

Getting Around

  • Trenitalia / Italo: Book 1-2 months ahead for the cheapest fares. High-speed trains connect all major cities in under 2 hours.
  • Open return for day trips: Regional trains (like Florence ↔ Pisa) don't need advance booking. Just buy at the station.
  • Venice vaporetto: Get a 24h or 48h pass. Individual rides are EUR 9.50 each and add up fast.

Booking Attractions

  • The Last Supper: Book 2-3 months in advance on the official site. Slots sell out immediately.
  • Vatican: Book the earliest morning slot. By 10am the queues wrap around St. Peter's Square.
  • Uffizi: Go on a weekday if possible. We went on a Sunday and it was still manageable in winter.
  • Colosseum: The Attico (top level) ticket is worth the extra cost for the view.

Money

  • Exchange rate: At the time of our trip, EUR 1 ≈ SGD 1.42.
  • Card vs. cash: Card accepted almost everywhere. Keep some cash for small shops and markets.
  • Tipping: Not expected in Italy. Service charge (coperto) is usually included in the bill.

Winter Travel Perks

  • Shorter queues at every major attraction.
  • Hotel prices 30-50% lower than summer peak.
  • Christmas markets and festive decorations everywhere.
  • Cooler weather (5-12°C) is actually perfect for walking 20,000+ steps a day.
